Computational Spectroscopy & Machine Learning

Deconvolving complex FTIR spectra using Principal Component Analysis.

The Challenge

Interpreting FTIR (Fourier-Transform Infrared) spectroscopy data is often hindered by overlapping peaks, making visual quantification highly subjective. The goal of this project was to establish a mathematical foundation to identify underlying chemical patterns and automatically sort complex biological samples (Okra mucilage).

Methodology

I developed a robust Python pipeline using SciPy and Scikit-Learn[cite: 2940, 2947]. The data underwent stringent preprocessing, including Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay filtering for derivative analysis[cite: 3128, 3555].

I deployed a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) algorithm to compress the high-dimensional spectral data and extract the most significant sources of chemical variance[cite: 3947].

Interactive PCA Clustering Results

The PCA model successfully sorted 6 samples into distinct, non-random clusters without human intervention. The primary axis (PC1, capturing 88.6% of variance) perfectly separated Seed samples from Pericarp samples[cite: 4045, 5081].

Next Steps: Predictive Modeling

Having proven the mathematical separability of the samples, the next phase involves training predictive algorithms. I have architected both a Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression to predict Polysaccharide Yield [cite: 4054], and a Neural Network (built in Keras) tailored to predict Antibacterial Inhibition (MIC) to validate theoretical hypotheses[cite: 4471].
