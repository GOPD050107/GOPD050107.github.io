Qualitative Fractography & Failure Analysis

Optical microscopy analysis of fracture principles and microstructural impacts[cite: 5102, 5104].

Executive Summary

This analysis examined the fractography features of glass, ceramics, and polycrystalline materials to understand core fracture principles. Six distinct samples were analyzed to determine how microstructural properties dictate fracture patterns[cite: 5102, 5103, 5104].

Methodology

Samples were observed using an Olympus SZ51 stereo microscope and a Fiber-Lite MI-150 external light source[cite: 5112]. A secondary dataset of images, captured using a Leitz Wild M3Z stereo microscope at magnifications ranging from 65X to 400X, was utilized for comparative analysis[cite: 5116, 5117].

Key Findings

1. Amorphous vs. Crystalline Fracture Behavior

The analysis revealed distinct differences based on material homogeneity. Glass, being amorphous and isotropic, exhibited explicit, predictable fracture markings like prominent hackle lines and smooth, shiny surfaces[cite: 5192, 5199]. In contrast, ceramic materials (like the ceramic square rod) demonstrated complex microstructures that obscured these markings, resulting in rougher fracture surfaces[cite: 5182, 5192].

2. Fractographic Features Identified

  • Origins: Initiation points were clearly identifiable in the glass rod and the reflected broken glass rod[cite: 5120, 5152].
  • Hackle Lines: Present across all materials, but most visible in glass samples, indicating crack propagation direction[cite: 5105, 5181].
  • Wallner Lines & Conchoidal Patterns: Observed in the reflected broken glass rod, confirming a brittle fracture mode[cite: 5155, 5156].
  • Cleavage Steps: Identified in the ceramic square rod SEM image, characteristic of brittle fracture in ceramics[cite: 5183, 5184].
